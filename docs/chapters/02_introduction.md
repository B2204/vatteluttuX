## 1.1 Background of the Study

### 1.1.1 The Tamil Language — A Living Ancient Treasure

Tamil is one of the oldest living languages in the world. It is a classical Dravidian language with a continuous literary tradition spanning over 2,500 years. The earliest known Tamil inscriptions date back to the 3rd century BCE, and the language has been in active use without interruption ever since. Unlike many ancient languages such as Latin, Sanskrit, or Old English — which are no longer spoken in daily life — Tamil is still the primary language of more than 80 million people in Tamil Nadu (India), Sri Lanka, Singapore, and diaspora communities around the world. In recognition of its antiquity and unbroken literary tradition, the Government of India declared Tamil a Classical Language in 2004, and UNESCO has recognized it as one of the world's long-surviving classical languages.

What makes Tamil especially remarkable from a historical perspective is the sheer volume of written records it has produced over the centuries. Tamil was written on a wide variety of surfaces — stone pillars, temple walls, rock faces, copper plates, palm leaves, and even pottery. These written records, known collectively as **inscriptions** (or "kalvettu" in Tamil, meaning "stone writings"), are primary sources of historical knowledge. They tell us about kings and queens, wars and treaties, land grants and tax systems, temple construction, trade routes, social customs, and religious practices over thousands of years.

### 1.1.2 The Vatteluttu Script — Round Letters of Ancient Tamil

Over its long history, Tamil has been written using several different scripts that evolved and changed over time. The major historical Tamil scripts include:

1. **Tamil-Brahmi** (3rd century BCE to 3rd century CE) — The earliest script used to write Tamil, derived from the Brahmi script of northern India.
2. **Vatteluttu** (3rd century CE to 12th century CE) — The "round letter" script that evolved from Tamil-Brahmi, characterized by its curved, flowing strokes.
3. **Pallava Grantha** (6th century CE onwards) — A script used primarily for writing Sanskrit texts in Tamil-speaking regions.
4. **Modern Tamil** (12th century CE to present) — The script in use today, which gradually replaced Vatteluttu.

Among these, **Vatteluttu** (வட்டெழுத்து, literally meaning "round letters" or "circular script") holds a particularly important place. The name comes from the distinctive rounded shapes of its characters, which were well-suited for carving on stone surfaces with a chisel. Vatteluttu was the dominant script used by the three great Tamil dynasties — the **Cholas**, **Pandyas**, and **early Pallavas** — for official inscriptions, royal decrees, and temple records from approximately the 3rd to the 12th century CE. During this 900-year period, Vatteluttu was the script of governance, religion, and daily record-keeping across Tamil Nadu and parts of modern-day Kerala.

The Vatteluttu script contains a total of **247 distinct character forms**, organized into the following categories following Tamil grammar:

| Category | Tamil Name | Count | Description |
|----------|-----------|-------|-------------|
| Vowels | உயிர் எழுத்து | 12 | Independent vowel characters (அ, ஆ, இ, ஈ, etc.) |
| Aytham | ஆய்தம் | 1 | A special character (ஃ) used in ancient Tamil |
| Pure Consonants | மெய் எழுத்து | 18 | Consonants with inherent "pulli" (dot) marker |
| Consonants | — | 18 | Base consonant forms |
| Compound Characters | உயிர்மெய் | 198 | Consonant + vowel combinations |
| **Total** | | **247** | |

The 198 compound characters (Uyirmei) are formed by combining each of the 18 consonants with each of the 11 vowel modifiers (the vowels excluding their independent forms). This systematic structure reflects the highly logical phonetic foundation of the Tamil writing system.

### 1.1.3 Why These Inscriptions Matter

The thousands of Vatteluttu inscriptions scattered across South India are not merely historical curiosities. They are the primary documentary evidence for an entire era of Tamil history. Without these inscriptions, our understanding of the Chola, Pandya, and Pallava kingdoms would be severely limited. Specifically, these inscriptions provide:

- **Political History**: Records of kings, their lineages, military conquests, and administrative structures. Many important Chola kings are known primarily through their inscription records.
- **Economic Data**: Tax records, land grants, trade regulations, and descriptions of agricultural practices reveal how the ancient Tamil economy functioned.
- **Religious Practices**: Temple foundation inscriptions, records of donations to temples, and descriptions of religious rituals provide insight into the spiritual life of ancient Tamil society.
- **Social Structure**: Records of different social groups, their occupations, and their interactions help reconstruct the social fabric of ancient Tamil society.
- **Linguistic Evolution**: The inscriptions provide a timeline of how the Tamil language itself changed and evolved over centuries, which is invaluable for linguists.

### 1.1.4 The Crisis of Inaccessibility

Despite their immense value, the vast majority of Vatteluttu inscriptions remain unread and untranslated. The fundamental reason is simple: Vatteluttu is a dead script — it is no longer used in everyday writing. Over time, Modern Tamil script evolved and completely replaced Vatteluttu starting around the 12th century CE. Today, only a very small and shrinking group of trained specialists — known as **epigraphists** — have the expertise to read and interpret Vatteluttu characters. Most Indian universities do not offer dedicated courses in Vatteluttu reading, and the few experts who exist are concentrated in specialized archaeological and linguistic research institutions.

This creates a severe bottleneck. The Archaeological Survey of India (ASI) and the Tamil Nadu State Department of Archaeology have catalogued and photographed thousands of inscriptions, but the actual work of reading, transcribing, and publishing these inscriptions proceeds at a painfully slow pace. Each inscription can take a trained epigraphist several hours or even days to decipher, especially when the stone surface is weathered, cracked, or partially damaged. At the current rate, it could take many more decades to clear the existing backlog — and new discoveries continue to add to it every year.

As these experts age and retire, the ability to access the knowledge locked within these inscriptions is at serious risk of being lost forever. This creates an urgent need for technological solutions that can assist in or automate the reading process.

## 1.2 Problem Statement

The core problem this project addresses can be stated simply:

**There is no automated tool available that can read Vatteluttu script from photographs of stone inscriptions and convert the recognized characters into Modern Tamil text.**

While OCR technology has advanced rapidly for modern languages — you can scan a printed English or Tamil page and get perfectly accurate digital text in seconds — ancient and historical scripts have received very little attention from the technology community. Vatteluttu, in particular, presents several unique challenges that make it much harder than modern text OCR:

1. **Script Complexity**: The Vatteluttu writing system contains 247 distinct character forms. These include 12 vowels, 18 consonants, 1 special character (Aytham), and 216 compound characters (Uyirmei) formed by combining consonants with vowels. Many of these compound characters look extremely similar to each other — the only visual difference may be a tiny diacritic mark positioned near the top or bottom of the main consonant stroke.

2. **Image Quality**: Stone inscriptions are exposed to centuries of weathering. Rain, wind, moss, and human activity gradually erode the carved surfaces. Photographs of these inscriptions often suffer from shadows, uneven lighting, surface cracks, and low contrast between the carved characters and the stone background.

3. **No Standard Spacing**: Unlike modern printed text, ancient inscriptions were not written with fixed spacing between characters. Characters are often placed very close together, making it difficult for automated systems to determine where one character ends and the next begins.

4. **Lack of Training Data**: There is no large, publicly available dataset of labeled Vatteluttu character images. Previous research efforts used small datasets covering only a fraction of the full character set.

5. **Limited Prior Research**: While OCR for modern scripts is a well-studied field, Vatteluttu has received very little attention from the research community. The few existing studies worked with only a small subset of characters (typically 28 out of 247).

These challenges collectively make Vatteluttu OCR a hard and relatively unexplored problem — and one that is worth solving because of the immense cultural and historical value of the inscriptions.

## 1.3 Objectives of the Project

The primary objectives of this project are as follows:

1. **To develop a deep learning-based OCR system** capable of automatically recognizing all 247 Vatteluttu character forms from photographs of stone inscriptions.

2. **To build a robust image preprocessing pipeline** that can clean and enhance inscription photographs affected by noise, uneven lighting, surface damage, and low contrast.

3. **To implement an automated character segmentation module** that can isolate individual characters from a processed inscription image, even when spacing between characters is inconsistent.

4. **To train a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) classifier** using a comprehensive synthetic dataset, achieving high classification accuracy across all 247 character classes.

5. **To create a complete bidirectional mapping database** that links each Vatteluttu character code to its corresponding Modern Tamil Unicode character, enabling automatic translation of recognized text.

6. **To deploy the entire system as a user-friendly web application** where researchers, historians, and the general public can upload inscription photographs and receive translated text without any technical expertise.

7. **To store recognition history** in a MySQL database, allowing users to review and manage their past recognition sessions.

## 1.4 Scope of the Project

The scope of this project covers the entire pipeline from image input to translated text output. Specifically, the project includes:

- **Image preprocessing**: Grayscale conversion, adaptive binarization, morphological noise removal, and contrast enhancement.
- **Character segmentation**: Connected Component Analysis (CCA) to detect and isolate individual characters based on spatial connectivity.
- **Character classification**: A ResNet-inspired deep CNN model trained on 247 character classes using a synthetically generated dataset.
- **Character mapping**: Automatic conversion of recognized character codes to Modern Tamil Unicode characters.
- **Web application**: A full-stack application with a React.js frontend (with TypeScript) and a FastAPI backend (Python).
- **Database integration**: MySQL database (via SQLAlchemy ORM) for storing recognition history with complete session details.
- **API design**: RESTful API endpoints for image upload, character recognition, history retrieval, and character map queries.

## 1.5 Limitations of the Project

While VattalettuX represents a significant step forward, the current version has certain limitations that should be acknowledged:

1. **Single Character Recognition**: The system currently recognizes characters individually. It does not perform word-level or sentence-level recognition. Each character is classified independently without considering the context of neighbouring characters.

2. **Synthetic Training Data**: The model was trained entirely on synthetically generated images. While the synthetic data was designed to be realistic (with stone-like textures, noise, and carving-like effects), real inscriptions may have visual properties that the synthetic data does not fully capture.

3. **Segmentation Sensitivity**: The character segmentation module works well when characters are reasonably spaced. However, for very closely packed or overlapping characters — which are common in certain inscription styles — segmentation accuracy decreases.

4. **No Language Model**: The system does not include a language model that could detect and correct errors based on Tamil linguistic rules. For example, if the model misrecognizes a character, there is currently no mechanism to flag or correct it based on the surrounding text.

5. **CPU-Based Inference**: The current deployment runs on a standard CPU. While the processing time of approximately 1.8 seconds per image is acceptable for most use cases, GPU-accelerated inference would significantly reduce this for batch processing scenarios.

## 1.6 Overview of the Proposed System

**VattalettuX** is an end-to-end OCR system that takes a photograph of a Vatteluttu stone inscription as input and produces the corresponding Modern Tamil text as output. The system is designed as a modular pipeline with four clearly defined stages:

**Stage 1 — Image Preprocessing**: The raw photograph is converted to grayscale, binarized using adaptive thresholding (to handle uneven lighting), cleaned using morphological opening (to remove surface noise), and enhanced using histogram equalization (to maximize contrast).

**Stage 2 — Character Segmentation**: The cleaned binary image is analyzed using Connected Component Analysis. Each connected group of foreground pixels (representing a potential character) is detected, bounded by a rectangle, and filtered based on size and aspect ratio constraints. Valid character regions are cropped and resized to a uniform 64×64 pixel format.

**Stage 3 — CNN Classification**: Each 64×64 character image is passed through a ResNet-based deep neural network with four residual blocks, batch normalization, global average pooling, and a 247-class softmax output layer. The model predicts the most likely Vatteluttu character class for each input, along with a confidence score.

**Stage 4 — Modern Tamil Mapping**: The predicted character class codes (such as `va_037`) are looked up in a JSON-based bidirectional mapping database to retrieve the corresponding Modern Tamil Unicode character. The final output is presented to the user as readable Modern Tamil text.

The entire system runs on a web application. The frontend, built with React.js and TypeScript, provides an intuitive drag-and-drop interface for image upload, real-time visual feedback with bounding box overlays on detected characters, and a clear display of recognition results with confidence scores. The backend, built with Python FastAPI, handles all computation and serves RESTful API endpoints. A MySQL database stores the history of all recognition sessions for future reference.
